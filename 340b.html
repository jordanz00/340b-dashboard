Why this matters

340B needs attention

For more than 30 years, the 340B program has helped hospitals serving vulnerable communities manage rising prescription drug costs. Despite HRSA oversight and a proven record, program critics want to scale it back — and several manufacturers have unlawfully stopped discounts for 340B drugs through contract pharmacies.

  • Manufacturers undermining the program: Several large drug companies have unilaterally stopped providing 340B discounts through community and specialty pharmacies — violating the statute.
  • Oversight imbalance: HRSA audits 200 covered entities annually, but only five drug manufacturers — we need parity.
  • State protection gap: Only 21 states have enacted contract pharmacy protection laws; Pennsylvania is in progress.

HAP advocates to protect 340B for all Pennsylvania providers, ensure the program continues to stretch limited resources and serve more patients, and thwart manufacturers’ unlawful changes — including efforts to convert 340B from an upfront discount to a back-end rebate model.

Overview

What is 340B?

Section 340B of the Public Health Service Act requires pharmaceutical manufacturers participating in Medicaid to sell outpatient drugs at discounted prices to eligible providers caring for uninsured and low-income patients. It is an upfront discount, not a rebate — and it costs taxpayers nothing. 340B allows hospitals to stretch limited federal resources and expand health services to patients and communities.

Program

Who qualifies

  • Federal grantee organizations
  • Critical Access Hospitals (CAHs), Sole Community Hospitals (SCHs), Rural Referral Centers (RRCs)
  • Public and nonprofit Disproportionate Share Hospitals (DSH) serving low-income populations
  • Children’s and cancer hospitals
  • Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)

Accountability

Oversight gap

340B hospitals must recertify annually, participate in HRSA and manufacturer audits, and maintain auditable records. Yet oversight is uneven: HRSA audits 200 covered entities every year, while only five drug companies are audited annually. HAP and AHA support parity in oversight so drug companies are held to the same accountability as providers.

Impact

Community benefit

The problem

Access at risk

Manufacturers are unlawfully limiting 340B access. Several large drug companies have unilaterally stopped providing discounts for 340B drugs dispensed through community and specialty pharmacies — violating the 340B statute. Others are pushing to convert the program from an upfront discount to a back-end rebate, which would require cash-strapped providers to finance drugs upfront, complicate patient access, and usurp HRSA’s oversight. Rural and underserved patients depend on contract pharmacies for high-cost drugs.

Pennsylvania

PA safeguards: no duplicate discounts

HAP supports program integrity and compliance. Pennsylvania’s safeguards include:

  • Manufacturers need not give both a 340B discount and a Medicaid rebate on the same drug (no duplicate discounts).
  • DHS maintains the 340B Drug Exclusion List and invoices manufacturers; covered entities may not dispense 340B-priced drugs on the list.
  • Contract pharmacies use non-340B drugs for MA beneficiaries; DHS invoices manufacturers for those rebates.

HAP opposes efforts to scale back 340B, impose excessive reporting burdens, or expand regulatory burden — and supports expanding the program to reach more vulnerable communities.
